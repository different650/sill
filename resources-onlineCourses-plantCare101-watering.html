Plant Care 101

Get a better grasp of the fundamentals with this introductory course to plant care. We'll cover the basics: light, water, and soil, so your plants stay happy and healthy.

Watering

In addition to being essential to photosynthesis, water provides structural support for plants.

This is called turgor pressure and you can think of it like a water balloon.

  • When the plant has sufficient water it stands stiff and upright. The leaves are perky and stems are supple.
  • When the plant has too little water the plant's structure is compromised. Leaves and stems will wilt, droop, curl or wrinkle.

Water is also crucial for nutrient uptake as minerals are only readily available for plants to absorb when dissolved in water as soluble salts.

  • Due to the typically high chlorine content of tap water, it may be advisable to use filtered water for more sensitive plants. Alternatively, let water sit for 24 hours or even in direct sunlight which helps to get rid of chlorine. Filtering water also removes vital minerals, so regular fertilizing will be necessary.
  • Best to not use solely distilled water which is chemically inert so it wouldn't add some essential minerals like calcium and magnesium that plants can get from tap water, and can also leach out the essential nutrients present in soil if a planter has a drainage hole! Better to use distilled water in intervals (1 month of just watering with distilled water).
  • When watering, always use warm/tepid water rather than cold or hot water which can harm and shock the roots.

Watering Basics

The average watering cadence for house plants is every 1-2 weeks depending on the plant's needs and environmental conditions.

Seasonal Watering Patterns

As seasons change so do the environmental factors that affect your plant, such as exposure to sunlight, temperature and humidity.

  • Less sunlight is available, plants use less water.
  • Colder temperatures mean potting mix will take longer to dry out.
  • More sunlight causes plants to photosynthesis using more water.
  • Water evaporates out of the soil.
  • Plants lose water through sweat or transpiration.

Basics

All plants want to dry out to some extent between watering. Understanding the plants' native habitat can help you to recreate the potting mix and environmental conditions it needs to thrive. For instance, a desert succulent will need water every 2-3 weeks compared to a rainforest Calathea that will need water every week.

  • The more light and/or heat the plant is exposed to, the more water it will need (and vice versa); which is why we increase watering during the summer and decrease in winter.
  • The size of the plant also affects it's watering needs. Plants with a larger volume of potting mix will hold water for longer and thus don't need to be watered as frequently as smaller plants that don't retain as much water.

When To Water

  • Visually inspect your plant for signs of underwatering.
  • Check the potting mix using your finger, a chopstick or moisture meter to see if the potting mix is dry at the root level.
  • Check the weight of the plant. Saturated potting mix will be much heavier than dry mix.

When To Water

Signs of underwatering can include:

  • Curling leaves
  • Drooping stems
  • Wrinkling or wilting leaves
  • Fading
  • Potting mix looks dry and is pulling away from the edges of the pot.

Potting mix is a sponge that holds water, but when it is too dry it's not absorptive. Water slowly, a little at a time or use the bottom watering method to give it time to fully saturate.

How To Water

Afer determining that the plant is sufficiently dry, use room temperature water to fully saturate the potting mix.

  • Water the potting mix evenly around the plant. Avoid splashing onto leaves which could cause fungal or bacterial spots.
  • Water up to ¼ to ⅓ of the volume of your planter. For planters with no drainage hole, be especially mindful of how much water you're using.
  • For planters with drainage, water freely until you see it drain out of the bottom. Let the water sit for 15-30 minutes or until the potting mix is fully saturated, then discard any excess water.

How To Water

Aerating soil (making perforations in the soil using a pencil or chopstick) helps to break up compacted soil, especially for those that like to dry out between waterings, which will help to evenly saturate the soil completely.

Watering Overview

  • Check plants before watering to ensure they are appropriately dry. Visually inspect the leaves and soil or physically check the soil.
  • Water the potting mix not the leaves with tepid water until the potting mix is evenly saturated.
  • In pots without drainage holes, give the plant ¼ - ⅓ the pot's volume-worth of water.
  • In pots with drainage holes, water until you see the excess drain out the bottom. Let it sit for about 15-30 minutes to soak up, then discard excess.
