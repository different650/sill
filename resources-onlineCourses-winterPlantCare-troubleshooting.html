Winter Plant Care

In this course, we're talking about all things winter and how you should care for your plants now that the temperatures have dropped and the days are shorter.

Leaf Drop

It will be completely natural to notice leaf drop as light levels fluctuate to lower intensity and duration in the Winter.

A few here and there will be natural, but if your plant is rapidly defoliating you may need to reassess your environment:

  • Consider increasing light by moving your plant closer to a window or incorporating artificial light.
  • Be mindful of drafts from hot/cold drafts which can also cause excessive leaf drop.
  • Make sure you're watering accordingly as over/under watering can also contribute to leaf loss.

Phototropism

Phototropism is a plants natural response to grow towards a light source. When a plant is begging for more light it'll tend to etiolate and stretch farther towards it hoping to get more!

While it's a harmless symptom, if it's due to lack of light it could make a plant weaker and less vigorous.

  • Rotate planters regularly for even growth for those that are directly in windows.
  • For those that are far from a light source it'll be best to increase light.

Colors fading

Some plants have adapted to brighter light conditions by making pigments or variegation. These pigments or mutations are energetically expensive for plants to make.

If your pigmented or variegated plants aren't getting enough light in the Winter their new growth will revert back to green! If noticed:

  • Increase light by moving your plant closer to a window or incorporating artificial light.
  • Prune off reverted green growth back to colored growth if needed for even color.

Colors Fading

Any variegated plants can fall under this category (marble pothos, variegated peace lily, variegated Monstera, etc.) as well as any pigmented plants like the Ficus burgundy, hoya tricolor, tradescantia, etc.

As you can see, Winter is a get through season, patience is key and just wait for those days to get longer!
