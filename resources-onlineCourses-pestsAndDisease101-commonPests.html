Pests And Disease 101

Learn about common pests and plant diseases, how to prevent them, and what to do to treat them when they occur.

Mealybugs

Mealybugs are soft-bodied, segmented oval-shaped insects covered with white powdery, waxy secretions.

They feed off of plant sap, and are primarily found on new growth. Mealybugs are able to move from plant to plant and adults can live up to 2 weeks in the home without a host!

Treatment: Spot treat visible adults with a q-tip or cloth dipped in rubbing alcohol. Follow up with neem oil or insecticidal spray.

Mealybugs

Continuous and overlapping generations make control difficult once mealybugs become established on a plant. The immature crawlers mature in about 6–9 weeks. Mature females die after egg laying. Unhatched eggs or young nymphs sometimes remain inside the protected cottony sac during unfavorable conditions.

Scale

Scale insects are rather unique in that they are immobile for most of their life cycle. They feed off a plant sap and primarily congregate on new growth.

In both hard and soft scales, the shell-like covering makes them impervious to insecticides and the females lay live young under the protection of their shells.

Treatment: Scrape/pick off all visible scale and follow with neem oil or insecticidal spray. If scale reappears, scrape them off again and repeat treatment.

Scale

Some forms produce honeydew, a sticky secretion that draws ants or fungal diseases to the plant. Newborn scales, called the crawlers, occur at first under the protective cover the adult, but soon leave to find a new place to feed. The crawler stage is only active for a short time, but it is this stage that is most susceptible to insecticidal treatment.

Spider Mites

Spider mites are primarily ound on new growthf where they can become easily airborne and blown to new hosts! They don't feed on plant sap. Instead, they rip open individual plant cells and drink the cell “guts"

They proliferate in hot, dry conditions. At first, you'll see a slight flecking or stippling (chlorotic/white spot) on the leaves. As mite feeding continues, leaves can drop from the plant.

Treatment: Wash plant in shower if possible first; spray with insecticidal soap or spinosad with Spider Mites listed as a target pest. Raising humidity can help slow down reproduction.

Spider Mites

Small arachnids that feed in plants and are one of the more destructive pests. When high populations develop, fine webbing is extensive. Adult females can live for about one month and lay ~100 eggs and do not have to mate to reproduce. The life cycle from egg to adult can vary from 7–21 days depending on temperatures.

Thrips

Thrips are slender insects with fringed wings that feeds off cell guts found on any part of the foliage. The collapse of plant cells due to feeding often results in deformed flowers, leaves, and shoots.

Silvery flecked scars/ "CD scratching" or small black "fecal" spots also may be seen on the expanded leaves which indicates Thrips are present.

Eggs can be laid on the upper or lower leaf surface,and even inserted into plant tissue.

Treatment: Keep plant away from others. Wash plant in shower if possible first; spray with insecticidal soap or neem oil.

Thrips

Adults are too weak to fly so they usually just hop when disturbed. When possible, keep thrips-infested plants isolated in a separate area to avoid spread. Adult females live for approximately 30-45 days and lay 150-300 eggs that are inserted into plant tissue.

Fungus Gnats

Fungus gnats may be observed resting on the growing media surface or moving across lower leaves.

Adult females are attracted to excess fungi in the soil and lay their eggs nearby so the developing larvae have access to a fungal food source.

Treatment: Diatomaceous earth or Mosquito bits, let soil dry out between waterings, yellow sticky traps.

Sand or rice hulls can also be used as a topdressing.

Fungus Gnats

Fungus gnats are generally harmless to our plants as they only feed on decaying organic matter/excess fungi within the soil of our plants. It's only when the population has become extensive and the food source is not enough where they can feed on fine roots of the plant which is almost rare to see for houseplants and primarily seen in large crop productions.

Since Fungus gnats reproduce quickly and each adult can lay between 100-200 eggs it's best to start treatment on all plants at the first sight.
